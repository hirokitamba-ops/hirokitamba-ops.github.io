Author: Hiroki Tamba

Abstract

This paper develops the Tri-Layer Integrated Model (TLIM) as an analytical framework for examining three interconnected dimensions of contemporary institutional governance. The first layer concerns predictive classification and the production of causation: when institutional systems rely on predictive categories to allocate attention, resources, or intervention, the classification process actively shapes the evidentiary conditions from which causal accounts are subsequently constructed. The second layer addresses infrastructural accountability and distributed decision authority: in environments where operational power is delegated across algorithmic systems and procedural workflows, responsibility is reconfigured as a property of system design rather than individual intent. The third layer examines semantic infrastructure and cognitive governance, analyzing how classification standards, labeling systems, and evaluation metrics function as cognitive architectures that structure institutional perception and interpretation. TLIM integrates these three layers into a unified analytical structure, enabling systematic investigation of governance environments in which institutional power operates through distributed technical, organizational, and semantic systems.
